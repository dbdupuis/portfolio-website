All About Georgie

Georgie was created to be your single source of truth in the evolving world of finance. Georgie is meant to be your one-stop financial hub where you can check the balance of all your accounts, make in-person and online payments, transfer money between accounts or to a friend, and manage your budget.

Aside from giving you the freedom to delete finance apps that only serve one purpose, Georgie was created to help you reach financial freedom. Georgie’s mascot, and app namesake, will be with you on every step of your financial journey. He’ll help you create achievable goals, identify spending trends, forecast debt payoff, and more.

Over the course of 9 months, I turned the idea of Georgie into a reality. Read on to see how I did it.

Research

This project’s initial objective was to create a responsive web application that allows anyone to send, spend, and transfer money without using a physical debit or credit card and without having to visit a physical bank or store. Through competitive analyses, I discovered there’s a market opportunity for an all-in-one financial management solution for young adults in the United States between 22 and 32 years old.

With this in mind, I set out to learn more about Georgie’s potential users through user interviews and surveys. Through user research, I discovered there’s a large group of people in this age range who just don’t feel confident managing their money.

Finally, I was able to compile all of this research and data into something useful: Georgie's user persona Dominic!

Ideation & Wireframing

Keeping all of these traits in mind, I began mapping out everyday tasks that Georgie could help them accomplish. This is where the idea of a character came to me. Who said personal finance had to be dull and full of spreadsheets?

With these basic frameworks, I started creating low-fidelity wireframes to determine what the screens should look like. Because I envision Dominic and other Georgie users creating their budget when they sign up, the create budget flow has been replaced with a sign up/onboarding flow.

Using these low-fidelity wireframe sketches as my guide, I began creating mid-fidelity wireframes in Figma to see what elements could actually fit in the alloted space. I also used this as an opportunity to further flesh out new user onboarding and create the first functional prototype of Georgie.

User Testing

With my mid-fidelity wireframes in hand, I was ready to start conducting user testing to see how people like Dominic felt about Georgie’s setup.

I recruited 6 participants for moderated, remote user tests. During these 6 sessions, I was looking for issues in Georgie’s user experience. You can see a list of all the issues found in this spreadsheet. The 5 issues I chose to tackle immediately were:

  1. Users did not know what information was needed from them during the sign-up process. (High Severity)
  2. One user did not recognize the hamburger navigation menu in the top-left corner. (High Severity)
  3. Users did not know what stage of "Send & Request Money" they were at. (High Severity)
  4. Users did not expect to be redirected to the Send & Request Money home screen after completing the task in the user test requiring them to send money to a friend. (High Severity)
  5. When prompted to make a payment, users tried to access their credit cards on the accounts section of their dashboard. (Medium Severity)
